Introduction

Module 4 focuses on the creation of fractal trees using turtle graphics in Python. To aid us in our understanding, we are kindly provided a fascinating book entitled The Algorithmic Beauty of Plants, written by Przemyslaw Prusinkiewicz and Aristid Lindenmayer, which simultaneously melds the fields of mathematics and computer science with the study of biological structures. Interestingly, plant beauty is considered to fundamentally result from symmetries in the formations of living plant structures with passing time; developmental algorithms have thus been formulated as an ongoing attempt to describe complicated plant growth with relation to time. Self-similarity is a consequence of developmental processes, and is described by Mandelbrot as: When each piece of a shape is geometrically whole, both the shape and the cascade that generate it are called self-similar. Lindenmayer systems, more commonly known as L-systems, are the theoretical foundation for creating plant models; we can implement detailed L-systems using computer graphical modeling to enable realistic visualization of developmental processes in plant organisms. Computer graphics have become essential for analysis of biological structures in general; they enable scientists to validate findings and showcase relatively complicated outcomes in a simplified format, ideal for educating audiences at all intellectual levels and fields of study, even mathematically ignorant artists intrigued by fantastic patterns discovered throughout various scientific fields. Consequently, certain L-systems provide code for generating fractals, including the fractal trees relevant to this module.
